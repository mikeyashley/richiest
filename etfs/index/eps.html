EPS (WisdomTree U.S. LargeCap Fund) uses a fundamental weighting approach instead of market-cap weighting. The fund holds large-cap U.S. stocks but weights them by earnings rather than market capitalization. This creates a portfolio that overweights high-earnings companies and underweights low-earnings companies compared to traditional S&P 500-type indices.

EPS Explained: What It Is and Why It Matters

EPS is an exchange-traded fund that tracks the WisdomTree U.S. LargeCap Earnings Index. Instead of market-cap weighting (where Apple's 30% weight comes from its $3T market cap), EPS weights companies by their reported earnings. This means companies with higher profits get higher weights, regardless of their current market valuation.

That makes EPS different from S&P 500 ETFs like SPY or VOO — it's not a pure large-cap market proxy. Instead, it's a "fundamentals weighting" approach designed to capture the performance of profitable large-cap companies while potentially avoiding overvalued stocks that dominate market-cap indices.

Investors usually use EPS for three reasons:

  • Earnings-focused exposure: gain exposure to large-cap companies with strong profitability metrics.
  • Alternative weighting: diversify beyond market-cap weighting to potentially reduce concentration risk.
  • Value orientation: the earnings-weighted approach naturally underweights high-valuation, low-earnings stocks.

Managed by WisdomTree, EPS launched in 2006 and remains one of the largest earnings-weighted ETFs available.

The Richiest.com Final Verdict: Is EPS Right For You?

EPS delivers an alternative approach to U.S. large-cap indexing through earnings-weighted methodology. It's not a replacement for market-cap weighted ETFs like VOO — it's a complement for investors who want diversified weighting approaches.

For investors who believe in fundamental indexing or want to avoid the concentration risk of market-cap weighting, EPS is worth considering. But if you want the lowest possible fee and pure large-cap market exposure, VOO or SCHX are better choices.

Bottom line: Use EPS as a diversification tool within your equity allocation, not as a core holding. It's a niche strategy with different performance characteristics than market-cap weighting.
