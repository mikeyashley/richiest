The Bottom Line Up Front

Quick take: FDL offers a concentrated portfolio of 50 U.S. dividend leaders selected by Morningstar’s equity research team—a fundamentally driven approach that leans on analyst judgment rather than pure quantitative screens.

FDL (First Trust Morningstar Dividend Leaders Index Fund) tracks the Morningstar® Dividend Leaders Index, which targets companies with a history of sustainable dividends, strong financial health, and competitive advantages. It’s a “quality‑plus‑yield” ETF, not a yield‑maximizing fund.

FDL works best for investors who want dividend exposure with a fundamental overlay, trusting Morningstar’s equity analysts to avoid dividend traps and emphasize durable payers. If you prefer a purely rules‑based, transparent index or want broader diversification, other dividend ETFs may be a better fit.

FDL Explained: What It Is and Why It Matters

FDL is a fundamentally driven dividend ETF that relies on Morningstar’s equity research to select and weight holdings. Unlike purely quantitative dividend indexes, the Morningstar® Dividend Leaders Index incorporates analyst‑assessed metrics such as economic moat, financial health, and dividend sustainability.

What sets FDL apart is its hybrid approach: it uses quantitative screens (minimum five‑year dividend history, positive earnings, etc.) but also weights constituents by the total dollar amount of dividends paid—a measure that favors larger, more established companies. The result is a concentrated portfolio of about 50 stocks that Morningstar’s research team believes can sustain and grow their dividends.

FDL appeals to investors who want:

  • Fundamental quality overlay: A dividend strategy that considers business moats and financial strength, not just yield.
  • Concentrated, high‑conviction exposure: A focused portfolio of dividend leaders rather than a broad basket.
  • Analyst‑driven screening: Trust in Morningstar’s equity research to avoid dividend traps and emphasize durable payers.
  • Large‑cap tilt: Weighting by dividend dollars naturally favors mega‑cap companies with substantial cash flows.

Managed by First Trust, FDL combines the rigor of Morningstar’s research with the operational efficiency of an index ETF.

FDL: The Good, The Bad, and The Steady

FDL’s fundamental approach brings distinct advantages and drawbacks. Its reliance on Morningstar research adds a quality filter but also introduces opacity and concentration risks.

Pros Cons
Quality‑focused selection: Morningstar’s moat and financial health screens help avoid dividend traps and emphasize companies with sustainable payouts. Less transparent methodology: The index incorporates analyst judgment, making it harder to predict changes than a purely rules‑based index.
Large‑cap, blue‑chip tilt: Weighting by dividend dollars favors established mega‑caps with stable cash flows, reducing exposure to speculative high‑yielders. Concentration risk: With only about 50 holdings, FDL is more vulnerable to individual stock setbacks than broader dividend ETFs.
Dividend‑dollar weighting: Companies that pay the most total dividends get the largest weights, aligning the portfolio with actual cash‑flow generation. Higher expense ratio: At 0.45%, FDL is more expensive than many plain‑vanilla dividend ETFs, though still cheap relative to active funds.
Hybrid active/passive approach: Combines the discipline of an index with the fundamental insights of Morningstar’s equity research team. Potential style drift: Analyst‑driven adjustments could shift the portfolio’s character over time in ways that aren’t fully predictable.

Who Should Consider FDL?

FDL makes the most sense for investors who want dividend exposure with a fundamental quality screen and are comfortable with a concentrated, large‑cap‑heavy portfolio. It’s designed for those who trust Morningstar’s research to identify durable dividend payers.

Best for: investors who value fundamental analysis alongside dividend income, prefer a focused portfolio of blue‑chip names, and are willing to pay a slightly higher fee for the research overlay.
Not ideal for: investors who want maximum transparency (rules‑based indexes), broad diversification, or the lowest possible expense ratio.
Main tradeoff: you get a quality‑filtered dividend portfolio, but you accept concentration risk and less predictable index changes.

The Research‑Aware Investor

The Blue‑Chipper

The Quality‑Seeker

Common Use Cases

  • Supplement to a broad‑market ETF: Use FDL to increase dividend income while maintaining exposure to large, stable companies.
