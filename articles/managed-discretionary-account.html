What is a Managed Discretionary Account (MDA)?

Definition and Features of MDA

A Managed Discretionary Account (MDA) is a type of investment account where the investor grants a financial adviser the authority to make investment decisions on their behalf. This authority allows the adviser to buy and sell assets without prior approval for each transaction, streamlining the investment process and enabling quicker responses to market changes.

Key Features and Components of MDAs:

  • Discretionary Authority: The financial adviser has the discretion to make investment decisions.
  • Professional Management: Investments are managed by experienced financial advisers.
  • Tailored Strategies: Investment strategies are customized to meet the specific goals and risk tolerance of the investor.
  • Active Management: The portfolio is continuously monitored and adjusted as needed.

How MDA Works

Managed Discretionary Accounts operate through a structured process where both the MDA provider and the investor have defined roles and responsibilities.

Detailed Process:

  • Agreement: The investor and the MDA provider agree on investment objectives and risk tolerance.
  • Discretionary Authority: The investor grants the financial adviser discretionary authority to manage the account.
  • Implementation: The financial adviser implements the investment strategy, making buy and sell decisions.
  • Monitoring: The portfolio is regularly monitored and adjusted to ensure it aligns with the agreed objectives.

Roles and Responsibilities:

  • MDA Provider: Manages the investments, ensures compliance with regulatory requirements, and communicates with the investor.
  • Investor: Provides initial input on investment goals and risk tolerance, reviews periodic reports, and stays informed about the account's performance.

How to Set Up a Managed Discretionary Account

Steps to Open an MDA

Opening a Managed Discretionary Account (MDA) involves a series of straightforward steps. Here is a detailed guide to help you get started:

  1. Research Providers: Begin by researching various MDA providers to find one that aligns with your investment goals and preferences.
  2. Consultation: Schedule a consultation with the selected provider to discuss your investment objectives, risk tolerance, and any specific requirements you may have.
  3. Documentation: Complete the necessary paperwork, which typically includes an account application, risk assessment, and agreement forms.
  4. Funding the Account: Transfer the required funds into your new MDA.
  5. Granting Authority: Officially grant discretionary authority to the financial adviser to manage your account.
  6. Review and Confirmation: Review the investment strategy proposed by the adviser and confirm the setup.

Regulatory Aspects of Managed Discretionary Accounts

Requirements and Obligations Under ASIC Regulations

Managed Discretionary Accounts (MDAs) in Australia are subject to strict regulations set by the Australian Securities and Investments Commission (ASIC). These regulations ensure that MDA providers operate in a transparent and compliant manner, protecting investors' interests.

Key ASIC Requirements:

  • Licensing: MDA providers must hold an Australian Financial Services (AFS) license.
  • Disclosure: Providers are required to disclose all relevant information to investors, including fees, risks, and investment strategies.
  • Compliance: Regular compliance audits and adherence to ASIC's regulatory guidelines.

For more detailed information on ASIC regulations, visit the ASIC.gov.au.

Compliance with the Corporations Act for MDA Providers

The Corporations Act is a key piece of legislation governing MDAs in Australia. It outlines the obligations and protections for both MDA providers and investors, ensuring a fair and secure investment environment.

Obligations Under the Corporations Act:

  • Investor Protection: Ensures that investors' rights are safeguarded through strict compliance requirements.
  • Transparency: Mandates clear and comprehensive reporting to investors about their investments.
  • Risk Management: Requires MDA providers to implement robust risk management practices.

For a comprehensive overview of the Corporations Act, you can read more on the ASIC.gov.au.

Distinction Between Discretionary and Non-Discretionary Accounts

Understanding the differences between discretionary and non-discretionary accounts is crucial for investors to make informed decisions.

Key Differences:

  • Decision-Making: In discretionary accounts, the financial adviser makes investment decisions on behalf of the investor. In non-discretionary accounts, the investor retains control over all decisions.
  • Flexibility: Discretionary accounts allow for quicker responses to market changes, while non-discretionary accounts require investor approval for each transaction.
  • Management: Discretionary accounts benefit from professional management, whereas non-discretionary accounts rely on the investor's input and oversight.

How Does an MDA Differ from a Traditional Managed Account?

The primary difference between an MDA and a traditional managed account lies in the level of discretion given to the financial adviser. In an MDA, the adviser has the authority to make investment decisions without seeking the investor's approval for each transaction, whereas, in a traditional managed account, the adviser must obtain consent for each investment decision.
